Lynch & Graham Screener

Methodology

This screener runs two parallel valuation frameworks — Peter Lynch and Benjamin Graham — across the S&P 500, Dow 30, and Nasdaq-100 universe. Each framework outputs a fair value estimate and a buy/hold/avoid signal. A blended score combines both for easy sorting.

Peter Lynch Framework

Lynch's core idea is that a stock is fairly valued when its P/E ratio equals its earnings growth rate (PEG = 1). Adding the dividend yield gives the PEGY ratio for income-paying stocks. The screener uses the G+D method (growth plus dividends) as the primary Lynch signal and the PEG band method as a secondary signal.

Lynch Status

Column: Lynch_Lynch_Status — primary Lynch buy/hold/avoid signal based on the G+D fair value.

Strong Buy
Price is at or below 70% of Lynch fair value (EPS × (Growth% + Dividend%))
Buy
Price is at or below 100% of Lynch fair value
Hold
Price is at or below 130% of Lynch fair value
Avoid
Price is above 130% of Lynch fair value

PEG Band

Column: Lynch_Lynch_PEG_Band — secondary Lynch signal based on the conservative PEG fair value.

Strong Buy
Price is at or below 70% of conservative PEG fair value
Buy
Price is at or below the conservative PEG fair value (EPS × 0.8 × Growth%)
Hold
Price is at or below the standard PEG fair value (EPS × Growth%)
Avoid
Price is above the standard PEG fair value

PEG Status / PEGY Status

Columns: Lynch_PEG_Status and Lynch_PEGY_Status — classify the raw PEG and PEGY ratios.

Cheap
PEG (or PEGY) below 1.0 — stock trades below its growth-adjusted fair value
Reasonable
PEG (or PEGY) between 1.0 and 1.5 — fairly priced relative to growth
Rich
PEG (or PEGY) above 1.5 — stock trades at a premium to its growth rate

Company Categories

Column: Lynch_Lynch_Category — automatically assigned based on the EPS growth rate. Lynch defined six categories in One Up on Wall Street (Ch. 8); this screener uses a simplified three-category version covering the growth-stock spectrum.

Slow Grower
Growth below 10% — mature companies growing near GDP rate; Lynch expected modest, bond-like returns
Stalwart
Growth between 10% and 20% — large, steady compounders; Lynch expected 30–50% gains over several years
Fast Grower
Growth above 20% — Lynch's favourite category; small, aggressive enterprises that can return 10–100×, but carry the most valuation risk

Key Metrics

Lynch_PEG
P/E ratio divided by Growth%. Below 1.0 = cheap; above 1.0 = rich.
Lynch_PEGY
P/E ratio divided by (Growth% + Dividend%). Rewards income-paying stocks.
Lynch_Lynch_Score
(Growth% + Dividend%) divided by P/E. Inverse of PEGY — higher is better.
Lynch_FV_GplusD
G+D fair value: EPS × (Growth% + Dividend%). The primary Lynch fair value estimate.
Lynch_Lynch_BuyPrice
Category-adjusted buy price — a category-specific discount applied to the G+D fair value (EPS × (Growth% + Dividend%)). Positive Lynch_Lynch_Discount_Pct means the stock is trading below this price.
Lynch_Lynch_Discount_Pct
How far below Lynch_BuyPrice the stock is currently trading. Positive = trading below buy price (good).

Discount Factors by Category

The buy price applies a category-specific discount to the G+D fair value (EPS × (Growth% + Dividend%)), providing a margin of safety relative to Lynch's primary fair value estimate:

Slow Grower
Buy at 75% of G+D fair value
Stalwart
Buy at 80% of G+D fair value
Fast Grower
Buy at 70% of G+D fair value — additional discount for the higher execution risk in fast-growing companies

Sources

  • Peter Lynch, One Up on Wall Street (1989) — Ch. 8 (company categories), Ch. 13 (PEG ratio fair value rule)
  • Peter Lynch, Beating the Street (1993) — PEGY ratio (dividend-adjusted PEG)

Benjamin Graham Framework

Graham's core idea is to buy at a significant discount to intrinsic value — the "margin of safety." His formula adjusts fair value for the prevailing interest rate environment: higher rates reduce intrinsic value, lower rates increase it. The screener computes two Graham formula variants and uses the more conservative of the two as the fair value estimate.

Graham Status

Column: Graham_Graham_Status — classifies current price relative to Graham fair value.

Deep Buy
Price is at or below 60% of Graham fair value (40%+ margin of safety)
Buy
Price is at or below 80% of Graham fair value (20%+ margin of safety)
Watch
Price is at or below 95% of Graham fair value
Avoid
Price is above 95% of Graham fair value

Fair Value Formulas

Version A (Graham_Graham_VA): EPS × (8.5 + 2 × Growth%) × 4.4 ÷ AAA_Yield

8.5
Graham's no-growth P/E baseline — the P/E appropriate for a company with zero growth
2 × Growth%
Each percentage point of annual growth adds 2 points to the fair P/E
4.4
Historical AAA corporate bond yield when Graham wrote the formula (~1962)
AAA_Yield
Current Moody's AAA corporate bond yield, fetched live from FRED on each run

Version B (Graham_Graham_VB): EPS × (7 + Growth%) × 4.4 ÷ AAA_Yield

Version B uses a lower base P/E (7 vs. 8.5) and a single-multiple growth term instead of double — a more conservative variant of the same rate-adjusted formula.

Graham_Graham_FV = MIN(VA, VB) — always uses the more conservative of the two estimates as the definitive fair value.

Graham Discount

Column: Graham_Graham_Discount_Pct — measures how far the current price is below Graham fair value. A positive value means the stock is trading at a discount (good — potential margin of safety). A negative value means it is trading at a premium to fair value.

Important Caveat

Graham himself included a warning alongside this formula: "This material is supplied for illustrative purposes only… let the reader not be misled into thinking that such projections have any high degree of reliability." The formula appears briefly in Chapter 11 to illustrate why the market's growth expectations are often unjustified — it is not presented in the stock-selection chapters and carries no supporting qualitative checks. Use the Graham signals as a valuation reference, not a buy/sell prescription.

Sources

  • Benjamin Graham, The Intelligent Investor, revised edition (1973) — Ch. 11 (intrinsic value formula: V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g) × 4.4 / Y)
  • Growth rate is capped at 15% for the Graham formula, consistent with Graham's own suggestion that projecting growth beyond 15% is speculative.
  • Version B (base P/E of 7, single g multiplier) is a conservative variant of the same rate-adjusted formula; it is not directly from Graham's text.

Graham Defensive Investor Checklist

Graham defined criteria that a stock must meet to be suitable for a conservative "defensive" investor — one who prioritises safety of principal and a reasonable return. Each criterion earns one point; the total out of 8 is the defensive score.

Columns: DefensiveLabel (Pass / Borderline / Fail) and DefensiveScore (0–8).

Pass
Score ≥ 6 out of 8 — meets Graham's defensive investor standard
Borderline
Score 4–5 — partially meets the criteria; warrants closer review
Fail
Score < 4 — does not meet the defensive investor threshold

Eight-Point Checklist

From The Intelligent Investor, Ch. 14. Where the screener adapts Graham's original threshold, the original is noted.

# Criterion Pass Condition
1 Market capitalisation ≥ $2 billion (Graham's original: annual sales ≥ $100M in 1973 dollars)
2 Current ratio ≥ 2.0 (current assets ÷ current liabilities)
3 Long-term debt / equity ≤ 1.0 (Graham's original: long-term debt ≤ net current assets)
4 Earnings per share history Positive EPS in 8 of the last 10 fiscal years (Graham's original: every year for 10 years)
5 Dividend history Paid a dividend in 5 of the last 10 years (Graham's original: uninterrupted for 20 years — relaxed to reflect modern tech-heavy universe)
6 EPS growth (10-year) Cumulative growth ≥ 33% over 10 years (~3%/yr)
7 P/E ratio ≤ 15 based on 3-year average EPS
8 P/B ratio ≤ 1.5, or P/E × P/B ≤ 22.5

Distress Signals & FCFF Discounted Cash Flow

Phase 7 adds two distress-detection sub-scores to the Safety pillar (Piotroski F-Score, Altman Z″) and the FCFF DCF is a fourth Value sub-group. Distress scores influence Safety continuously. The separate is_trap and Trap_Reasons fields remain warning-only research prompts and never hard-veto a ticker. All band thresholds below are [ASSUMED] version-controlled config constants with no empirical anchor yet — monitor the live distribution on the Stats page and expect tuning iterations.

Piotroski F-Score

Column: Piotroski_F (0–9) — the standard nine-criterion test (Piotroski, 2000) comparing the current fiscal year against the prior year across profitability, leverage/liquidity, and operating efficiency:

Profitability (4 points)
Positive net income; positive operating cash flow; ROA improved year-over-year; operating cash flow exceeds net income (accruals quality)
Leverage, Liquidity & Source of Funds (3 points)
Long-term debt/assets decreased; current ratio improved; no new share issuance (shares outstanding did not increase)
Operating Efficiency (2 points)
Gross margin improved year-over-year; asset turnover improved year-over-year

The 0–9 raw score maps to the Safety sub-score via piecewise bands (distressed → 0–20, weak → 20–40, average → 40–65, strong → 65–85, very strong → 85–100). When the prior-year statements are unavailable, Piotroski contributes a neutral 50.0 to the Safety pillar rather than being skipped — this prevents stocks with thin data coverage from unfairly inheriting Safety scores from the remaining inputs alone.

Altman Z″-Score

Column: Altman_Z — the Z″ variant of Altman's bankruptcy-prediction model, designed for non-financial, non-manufacturing companies:

Z″ = 6.56·X1 + 3.26·X2 + 6.72·X3 + 1.05·X4

X1
Working Capital ÷ Total Assets
X2
Retained Earnings ÷ Total Assets
X3
EBIT ÷ Total Assets
X4
Book Value of Equity ÷ Total Liabilities

Z″ below 1.1 falls in the distress zone (Safety sub-score of 0.0); Z″ above 2.6 falls in the safe zone (Safety sub-score 70–100); values in between are linearly interpolated across the grey zone. Altman Z″ is excluded for Financial Services companies (the model is not designed for highly-leveraged balance sheets like banks and insurers) — excluded tickers contribute the same neutral 50.0 as missing-data tickers, never a zero.

Screen-Grade FCFF DCF and Reverse DCF

Columns include DCF_Intrinsic_Value, DCF_Value_Low, DCF_Value_High, DCF_Discount_Pct, DCF_Implied_Growth, DCF_Growth_Used_Pct, DCF_Growth_Gap_Pct, DCF_WACC_Pct, DCF_WACC_Unfloored_Pct, DCF_WACC_Floor_Applied, currency metadata, and dcf_reverse_converged.

Base free cash flow to the firm
Reported operating cash flow minus capital expenditure, plus after-tax interest expense. This converts the reported cash-flow starting point into a screen-grade FCFF estimate while retaining the working-capital movement already captured in operating cash flow. Missing capex produces no DCF rather than treating all operating cash flow as free cash flow.
Stage 1 — Growth fade
The reconciled growth estimate is bounded between −20% and 15%, then linearly fades over five years toward the terminal growth rate. EPS growth remains a proxy for FCFF growth at this screening depth and is disclosed as a model limitation.
Terminal value
Gordon Growth Model using a non-negative terminal rate capped at 3.0%. The dashboard also reports terminal value as a percentage of enterprise value so highly terminal-dependent results are visible.
Discount rate (WACC)
Cost of equity uses the live 10-year Treasury rate plus beta times the configured 5.5% equity risk premium. Pre-tax cost of debt is the higher of the live Moody's AAA yield and a usable observed interest-expense-to-average-debt rate. Equity and debt are market-capital-structure weighted and debt receives the tax shield. Missing beta defaults to 1.0 and produces an explicit data warning. Because a current capital structure can create implausibly low screen-level rates, effective WACC is no lower than the greatest of calculated WACC, the 10-year Treasury plus 2.5 percentage points, and terminal growth plus 4.0 percentage points. The raw rate and whether the guardrail was applied are both displayed; this is a screening control, not a company-specific target-capital-structure forecast.
Enterprise-to-equity bridge
Present value of FCFF produces enterprise value. Cash is added, total debt is subtracted, and the result is divided by diluted shares where available. If aggregate total debt is present it is used directly; current debt is added only when total debt must be reconstructed from components. If the quoted share price and reported financial statements use different known currencies, no DCF is produced: adding cash, debt, and enterprise value across unlike currencies would create a false per-share value. Missing currency metadata is retained with an explicit warning.
Valuation range
The low and high values pair changes in initial growth, WACC, and terminal growth. The base DCF alone feeds scoring; the range is displayed to make assumption sensitivity visible. If the stressed enterprise-to-equity bridge falls below net debt, the low common-equity value is reported as zero rather than as missing, consistent with limited liability, and an explicit warning is attached.
Forward discount
DCF_Discount_Pct = (1 − Price ÷ Intrinsic Value) × 100 — positive means the stock trades below its DCF intrinsic value (cheap signal), consistent with the Lynch/Graham discount sign convention used throughout the screener.
Reverse DCF
Solves for the growth rate that would justify the current price, given the same WACC and terminal-growth assumptions. DCF_Growth_Gap_Pct is the bounded screening growth assumption minus market-implied growth. When the solver cannot find a root (no valid growth rate produces the observed price), DCF_Implied_Growth is None and dcf_reverse_converged is False — never a silently clipped or default value.

This is an idea-screening diagnostic, not a company-specific research model. It is excluded for Financial Services and Real Estate (REITs). The former EPS projection remains available under explicitly named Discounted_Earnings_* columns for comparison, but it is not described as DCF and does not feed the DCF score. High leverage, terminal value above 85% of enterprise value, currency uncertainty, and other assumption risks remain warnings rather than hard stock-level gates.

Sector Applicability Matrix

Some metrics are not meaningful for every sector. Excluded metrics are always emitted as None (missing), never zero — a zero would imply "worst possible," which misrepresents "not applicable." Average-over-present scoring then handles the gap for that ticker's pillar the same way it handles any other missing input.

Sector Excluded Metrics Reason
Financial Services DCF, EV/EBIT, Altman Z″ FCF-based valuation and Z″ are not designed for bank/insurer balance sheets
Real Estate (REITs) DCF REIT cash generation is FFO-based, not FCF-based
All other sectors None Piotroski, Altman Z″, and DCF all apply

A ticker with an unknown or missing sector receives no exclusion (all metrics are attempted) — the matrix only excludes sectors it explicitly recognizes.

Data Sources

All data is fetched automatically on each screener run. No manual data entry is required. The screener runs on a weekday schedule via GitHub Actions.

Price, EPS history, dividends, balance sheet ratios
Yahoo Finance via the yfinance Python library
EPS growth (5Y CAGR), P/B ratio, market cap, current ratio, debt/equity
Finnhub — pre-calculated fundamentals; yfinance history is used as fallback when Finnhub data is absent
AAA corporate bond yield
FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data) — Moody's AAA series, fetched live on each run. Current value: loading…
Index constituents (S&P 500, Dow 30, Nasdaq-100)
Wikipedia — scraped on each run to keep the universe current

Growth is sourced from Finnhub's pre-calculated 5-year EPS CAGR where available, falling back to a computed CAGR from yfinance annual EPS history. Growth is capped at 25% for Lynch metrics and 15% for Graham metrics. Stocks with negative or zero growth cannot be valued by these formulas; rather than being dropped, they are retained but assigned the worst-possible value score so they rank at the bottom.
